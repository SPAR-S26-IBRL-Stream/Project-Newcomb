# IB Bandit — Pareto-DP demo for an Infra-Bayesian two-armed bandit

A self-contained companion notebook for the NeurIPS 2026 paper draft
*"IBRL from SPAR Project"* (the team draft is in the project Drive). The
notebook realises in code the core distinction the paper makes between
**classical Bayesian averaging** and **infra-Bayesian (IB) lower-expectation
evaluation**, on a small finite-outcome bandit where everything can be solved
exactly.

This is a stand-alone experiment — it does **not** depend on `ibrl/`. The
algorithms (Bayesian DP and Pareto DP) are implemented from scratch in the
notebook. It uses only `numpy` and `matplotlib`, both already in the project's
`pyproject.toml`.

## What the notebook shows

`ib_bandit_1.ipynb` implements a **Knightian-uncertainty bandit** as a worked
example of the architecture described in §2 and §4 of the paper (a-measures,
infradistributions, lower expectations, finite reductions for bandit-style
settings). The essential comparison is **Bayesian (50/50 prior over casinos)
vs IB (Knightian uncertainty over casinos), under an Omega adversary that
picks the casino after seeing the agent's policy**. The environment and
agents map onto the paper as follows:

| Paper concept | Notebook realisation |
|---|---|
| Infradistribution Ψ over outcome models | The set `{m_Red, m_Blue}` of two casino models |
| Lower expectation `inf_{a∈Ψ} a(f)` | `min(V_Red(π), V_Blue(π))` |
| Classical Bayesian agent (assumes realisability + grain of truth) | `solve_bayes_dp` — assumes Nature picks Red/Blue 50/50 |
| Infra-Bayesian agent (maximin) | `solve_pareto_dp` + `extract_policy` |
| "Omega" / adversarial selector (paper's worst-case environment) | The min over the two casinos at evaluation time |
| Finite extremal-point representation (paper §2.5) | The Pareto frontier of `(V_Red, V_Blue)` pairs |

### The setup

Two casinos that are exact mirror images:

|       | Arm 0 | Arm 1 |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Red   | Safe (p=0.5) | Risky (p ∈ {0.1, 0.9}) |
| Blue  | Risky (p ∈ {0.1, 0.9}) | Safe (p=0.5) |

Risky-arm probability is drawn from a bimodal prior `{0.1: 0.5, 0.9: 0.5}` so
its prior mean is 0.5 — arms are statistically indistinguishable at t=0, but a
single pull is highly informative. The agent does not see the casino colour.
Horizon `T=5`.

This is the Knightian-bandit experiment from §3.1 of the paper, restricted to a
two-environment infradistribution so the lower expectation reduces to a `min`
over two scalar values, which makes exact computation feasible and the
Pareto-DP visualisation interpretable.

## Algorithms

1. **Bayesian DP** — standard backward induction over sufficient statistics
   `(n0, s0, n1, s1)`. Linear objective ⇒ subproblems decouple, so Bellman
   recursion stores a scalar `V(s)` per state.
2. **Pareto DP** — the IB objective `min(V_Red, V_Blue)` is non-linear in the
   environment-conditional values, so subproblems are coupled. The notebook
   stores at each state the **Pareto frontier** of achievable
   `(V_Red, V_Blue)` pairs over all deterministic tail policies, and combines
   them by cross-product across `(success, failure)` successors. At the root,
   the IB-optimal point is `argmax_{(v_r,v_b)∈Frontier} min(v_r, v_b)`.
3. **Policy extraction** — backpointers stored on every frontier point let us
   backtrack from the root's IB-optimal point to recover the full deterministic
   policy.

## FAQ

This section answers questions raised on the team chat. The setup is small
but unusual, and several details are worth pinning down explicitly.

### What exactly is the environment? Multiple episodes or one?

There is **one episode of length `T`** (the notebook runs `T=5`; up to `T≈10`
is feasible with Pareto DP). At the start of the episode an environment is
fixed and stays fixed for the whole episode:

- one of two casinos, `Red` or `Blue`, is chosen by Omega;
- inside that casino, the Risky arm's true probability `p ∈ {0.1, 0.9}` is
  drawn once from the bimodal prior `{0.1: 0.5, 0.9: 0.5}`.

Both choices are **made once at `t=0` and never updated by the world after
that.** The agent then plays `T` Bernoulli pulls in the chosen casino.

### How does Omega choose? What "pair" does it pick?

Omega is the worst-case adversary. **Before the episode** Omega:
1. reads the agent's full deterministic policy (the function from history
   to next arm);
2. computes the agent's expected total reward under each casino,
   `V^Red(π)` and `V^Blue(π)`, marginalising over the Risky-arm prior;
3. commits to whichever casino gives the smaller of the two — i.e.
   realises the `min` in `min(V^Red, V^Blue)`.

Omega does **not** choose mid-episode and never re-decides. The "pair" that
gets fixed is `(casino, true Risky-arm probability)`, and the casino is the
adversarial half — the Risky probability is ordinary statistical
uncertainty.

### Why aren't the casinos indistinguishable to the agent?

At `t=0` they look identical (the prior mean of the Risky arm is `0.5`,
exactly equal to the Safe arm), so the *one-step* expected reward of either
arm is `0.5` in either casino. But over `T` steps the agent's policy is
asymmetric: it must commit to pulling either Arm 0 or Arm 1 first, and
that breaks the symmetry between the casinos. If the policy pulls Arm 0
first, that arm is Safe in Red and Risky in Blue — so the *trajectory
distributions* differ between casinos even though the first-step expected
reward does not. Omega exploits exactly this asymmetry.

### How does this deal with dynamic consistency / the affine $b$-term?

It doesn't need to. Dynamic consistency in the full IB theory matters when
the agent's belief over the *adversarial* / Knightian piece changes
mid-episode — the affine `b`-term is what carries the value of the
counterfactual branches forward so that past-self and present-self agree.

In this scenario, **Omega commits to the casino once at `t=0` and never
revises**, so there is no mid-episode update over the Knightian piece. The
only thing the agent updates during the episode is its posterior over the
Risky arm's probability *within* the chosen casino, which is ordinary
Bayesian updating and has no consistency problem.

So this notebook exercises the **policy-selection / maximin** side of IB
(the outer `inf` over the infradistribution) without engaging the IB update
rule for $a$-measures. The $b$-term would become essential if Omega could
revise mid-episode, or if the agent had to update its belief over the set
of casinos rather than within one fixed casino. That is the harder regime
the full theory is built for; the paper's §2.7 covers it but this notebook
does not implement it.

### Why use a Pareto DP instead of greedy maximin?

A greedy "pick the action that maximises `min(Q_Red, Q_Blue)` at each step"
does not learn — it can only see one step ahead and so it exploits whatever
arm currently looks safer, never investing in the informative pull that
would shrink Knightian uncertainty. The IB objective is non-linear in
per-environment values, so the principle of optimality fails for any
scalar Bellman recursion: at each state we need the full Pareto frontier
of `(V^Red, V^Blue)` tradeoffs available to the tail policy. The exact
algorithm is then backward induction on Pareto sets, which is what the
notebook implements. It is feasible at small `T` (`T=5` runs in
milliseconds; `T=10` should still be tractable) but does not scale
asymptotically. There is no fast general-purpose IB-bandit solver as far
as we know.

### Why is this called the "policy-selection side" of IB?

IB has two halves:
1. **The IB update** — how a set of $a$-measures is updated on observation
   (paper §2.7). When Omega is fixed at `t=0`, this collapses to ordinary
   Bayesian updating of the within-casino posterior, since the only thing
   to update is classical statistical uncertainty.
2. **Policy selection under the lower expectation** — finding `π` to
   maximise `inf_{a∈Ψ} a(V(π))`. With `Ψ = {m_Red, m_Blue}`, this is
   `argmax_π min(V^Red(π), V^Blue(π))`.

This notebook is purely about (2). A scenario where Omega cannot read the
full policy and instead has to *infer* it from observed behaviour would
force the agent to update over `Ψ` mid-episode, and the full IB machinery
including the $b$-term would be required.
